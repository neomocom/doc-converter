The Need for an Integrated Approach to Well-Being in Healthcare

Lawrence M. Lewis, MD, Evan Schwarz, MD, Randall Jotte, MD, and Colin P. West, MD

Colin P. West, MD, PhD, Professor of Medicine, Medical Education, and Biostatistics, is at the Mayo Clinic, Rochester, Minnesota
Corresponding author.

Physician “burnout” is not new, as suggested by a “Wellness for Emergency Physicians” handbook published by the American College of Emergency Physicians in 1995, and a highly cited editorial on the topic in the July 2001 Annals of Internal Medicine.1,2 Burnout is defined by the presence of one or more of three cardinal symptoms: 1) emotional exhaustion, 2) depersonalization, or 3) a sense of reduced personal accomplishment.3 Studies show physicians suffer more from burnout than the general U.S. working population and that this has gotten worse over the previous decade.4,5 Although there appears to be some tapering of this trend, a 2019 national survey found that overall, 44% of physicians suffer from burnout. Burnout was particularly prevalent in front-line fields such as emergency medicine, internal medicine, and family medicine as well as certain other specialty practices such as urology, neurology, and physical medicine and rehabilitation.6

In 2017, the National Academy of Medicine (previously the Institute of Medicine) launched the Action Collaborative on Clinician Well-Being and Resilience, which resulted in more than 60 organizations, including the American Medical Association, the Association of American Medical Colleges, and the Accreditation Council for Graduate Medical Education, signing on to study and address this issue.7 This culminated recently in the release of the National Academy of Medicine consensus report “Taking Action Against Clinician Burnout: A Systems Approach to Professional Well-Being (2019)”.

In recognition of these issues, over the past few years, several departments and divisions at Washington University School of Medicine hosted prominent guest lecturers to provide grand rounds in the area of physician wellness. These presentations were excellent, but resulted in almost no change in our approach to the problem. The solutions initially enacted were limited to mindfulness training and other activities to improve individual resiliency, suggesting that the individual was responsible for improving their own situation. Most faculty regard individual resiliency alone as insufficient to resolve burnout, as they view the problems as systemic and beyond individual coping mechanisms. In the fall of 2018, in an effort to address the issue of physician burnout in a more integrated way, we initiated interviews with several dozen stakeholders across a multitude of specialties to identify the major dis-satisfiers in our work environment.

These meetings culminated in an interdisciplinary symposium designed to highlight what we internally know about the problem, gain external insight, and set an agenda to address the issue across the entire medical center.8 What follows is a compilation of lessons learned from our experience and that of several healthcare organizations that pioneered efforts to improve well-being and restore joy in the practice of medicine. Much of the background data comes from the presentations at the symposium, particularly from our keynote speaker, Colin West, MD, Professor of Medicine, Medical Education, and Biostatistics at Mayo Clinic. Although the importance of the topic has become self-evident to many physicians, we begin with an argument of why people both inside and outside medicine should care about this issue.

Why Should We Care? The Consequences of Physician Burnout

Physician burnout is associated with a doubling in the self-reported rate of medical errors,9,10 as well as more than doubling the risk of reduced patient satisfaction scores.10 Additionally, physician burnout is also associated with reduced hours in the workforce and earlier retirement.11,12 This data not only has important consequences regarding patient safety and workplace reputation, it also suggests a financial cost through increased litigation in the former, and increased recruitment and training efforts in the latter. In fact, job turnover and reduced clinical effort from burnout are estimated to cost the healthcare system over 4.6 billion dollars a year, with total burnout-related costs rising much higher.13

Who is Most Affected?

Shanafelt et al. in a 2011 survey of over 7,000 physicians across 23 specialties found that the front-line specialties of emergency medicine, internal medicine, and family medicine, along with neurology had the highest rates of burnout.14 Medscape has been reporting on U.S. physician burnout since 2013. Results differ year to year regarding the specialty with the highest reported percentage of physician burnout, but again front-line specialties consistently rank in the top five.15 As demonstrated every year since the initial survey, women continue to have higher rates of burnout than men (50% vs 39%).6 Age also appears to be a risk factor for physician burnout. Several studies suggest younger physicians are at higher risk of burnout,16,17 although the aforementioned 2018 Medscape survey found that the percentage of physicians with burnout symptoms increased between the ages of 28 and 54, declining significantly in those older than 54 years.15

Where to Intervene: Leading Causes of Burnout Among Healthcare Providers

The 2017 and 2018 Medscape surveys also identified the four leading causes of burnout: 1) too many bureaucratic tasks; (2) spending too many hours at work; (3) lack of respect from administrators/employers, colleagues, or staff; and (4) the Electronic Health Record (EHR).5,15 These findings are consistent with what we found in dozens of interviews within our medical center, and are supported by several independent reports. It is noteworthy that the European Union extensively used EHRs for many years, but the burden of documentation is considerably less in the EU countries than in the U.S. (Figure 1).18

Based on several small surveys across multiple specialties at our medical center, we obtained nearly universal agreement on targeting three main areas: 1) Improving workplace efficiencies through re-design of processes and improved resourcing/training; 2) Improving communication between administration and faculty and between disciplines focusing on respect, collegiality, and shared decision-making; and 3) Improving transparency and incorporating shared decision-making in compensation and monetary decisions.

Initial Next Steps

The first step (number one below) was to form an interdisciplinary Wellness Task Force, which would identify and attempt to quantify the most problematic issues and recommend further analysis when needed, or partner with a wide variety of Washington University based resources to implement potential solutions.

  1. Identify faculty in all clinical departments and divisions with an interest in improving well-being in the workplace and who were designated by their Chairs or Chiefs to sit on the newly-formed interdisciplinary Wellness Task Force.

  2. Complete the purchase and implementation of a validated well-being survey (the Mayo Well-being Index) which all interested faculty members (of participating departments or divisions) can voluntarily and anonymously complete with immediate personal feedback regarding their score and how their score compares with national averages. The survey was tailored to allow 3 institution-specific questions, which will better inform our efforts to address major dis-satisfiers in our workplace environment.

  3. Reimplement a peer-to-peer program to help faculty cope with serious workplace issues affecting their well-being, in particular issues surrounding adverse outcomes and medical litigation.

  4. Identify the major dis-satisfiers affecting well-being across disciplines through departmental and divisional representatives, and through the responses to the well-being survey.

  5. Partner with the Washington University Clinical Assistance Team to identify and circumvent or improve the most cumbersome, time-consuming, and frustrating processes in our EHR.

  6. Review current resources, and in some cases recruit additional resources and personnel to help manage serious mental health or life issues among faculty

  7. Partner with Barnes-Jewish Hospital’s Patient Satisfaction and Patient Safety initiatives in a multidisciplinary committee to look at communication issues at all levels of interaction, including patients, nurses and support staff, and peer-to-peer consultative communications.

These efforts are just getting underway, as we attempt to identify resources both inside and outside our institution. We don’t know what impact, if any, they will have on restoring satisfaction and joy in our work. Yet, colleagues within the Wellness Task Force collectively sense a recognized appreciation among peers simply in addressing the issue, dedicating resources to further insights as to the nature of the problem, and a willingness to promote solutions. Our colleagues seem grateful this issue is being addressed. Until recently, a lack of wellness was regarded as a private, personal issue, not a consequence of systemic dysfunction. Those attitudes are changing. There will need to be a sustained effort to view any changes in practice policies and procedures through the lens of physician wellness, with shared decision-making and transparency in the major decisions that affect our daily practice. As more medical centers across the country embark on wellness programs, we will need to see what interventions are most efficient and effective in helping to reduce the high burnout rate affecting so many physicians across the entire geographic and demographic spectrum, and share this information broadly.

COVID-19: An Epilogue

As this comes to press, healthcare providers across this country are facing the frightening and daunting task of caring for large numbers of patients in what might become the worst pandemic in a century. In addition to the stress of preparing for a potential tsunami of patients who are likely to require resources we will not have, and eventually having to make difficult triage decisions regarding who gets care, we currently do not even have enough personal protective equipment for our own protection, and are losing large numbers of our workforce to quarantine precautions even as the need for healthcare providers increases. There is a real danger of overwhelming fatigue and an inability to provide the level of care we are accustomed to in this country if we do not develop a procedure to allow exposed but asymptomatic healthcare workers to return to work. Developing a uniform policy to support this effort while mitigating against potential spread may be the single most important thing any hospital or medical center can do; many physicians and nurses are already exhausted, and we are far from the peak.

On a positive note, this crisis may provide an opportunity to pilot programs and policies that may help restore the joy in practicing medicine once this crisis has resolved. The cooperation among various medical services is as high as many of us have ever seen it. There is a single focus that cuts across the various healthcare systems and multitude of specialty services which appears to have improved the satisfaction of many providers despite the enormous stress they are under. Appreciation, cooperation, streamlining unnecessary regulations and “red tape”, and reducing unnecessary tasks, so that the care of patients and the health of our community is once again the principle focus of our work may be a partial antidote to the burnout we have been witnessing over the past several years.

A unique challenge is presented by this current pandemic. With the advent of social distancing, and the closure of most areas of entertainment, our usual methods of socializing and replenishing ourselves for the next onslaught have been severely curtailed. We and others are experimenting with virtual happy hours and sharing interesting tv shows, movies, or books, particularly for those of us furloughed or isolated at home. Probably limiting how much time you spend thinking about COVID out of work will be a necessary break.

The current COVID-19 crisis is likely to get much worse in this country before it gets better, but it has already shined a spotlight on the critical importance of healthcare provider well-being (both physically and psychologically) which we hope outlasts the pandemic and results in long-term proposals to address the problem of physician burnout.
